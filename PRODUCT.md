# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

This repo is the CardOps marketing website, so the design rulebook is web (responsive mobile web included). The product it sells is a native **iOS** app; app-side design work should follow Apple HIG, but that lives in the separate iOS project, not here.

## Users

Full-time and serious trading-card dealers — the people who run tables at shows and conventions and depend on that income. Their context is the floor, not a desk: phone in hand, a line of buyers, and convention-hall wifi that drops when the room fills. The job to be done is to know what every card cost and what it's worth, sell and trade fast at the table, and walk away knowing the show's real profit. The page speaks to that dealer first; weekend and hobbyist sellers are welcome but not who the copy is tuned for.

## Product Purpose

CardOps is an iOS inventory app for trading-card vendors. Scan a card with the camera and on-device recognition matches it against a 20,000+ card catalog; track cost against current market value; run an offline-first Show Mode to search, sell, trade, and buy at the table; and see per-show sales, expenses, purchases, and profit. It exists because show vendors run a real business on spreadsheets, binders, and wifi that fails exactly when they need it. The app is pre-launch ("coming soon to the App Store"); this site's job right now is to build launch anticipation so serious dealers download it on day one.

## Positioning

The inventory app built for card vendors who sell at shows — offline-first, per-show profit, made for the table and not the desk. Every screen reinforces that this is a working tool for running a table, not a collection-tracking hobby app.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary CTA: App Store — the app is pre-launch, so the live CTA today is the "Coming soon to the App Store" state; at launch it becomes the App Store download. There is no notify/waitlist mechanism on the page yet; adding one would let anticipation convert to launch-day installs.
- Secondary CTA: direct contact (andre.havasi@icloud.com) for questions, bug reports, and feature requests.
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: *Scan, price, and sell trading cards at shows — even when the wifi dies.*
- Belief ladder (what a dealer must come to believe, in order): (1) this was built for my world — shows, tables, per-show P&L, not collection management; (2) it keeps working offline, so selling never stops when the hall wifi drops; (3) the scanner is fast and accurate enough to trust across a big catalog; (4) it beats the spreadsheet and binder I use now, giving real per-show profit instead of busywork; (5) my data stays mine — CSV in and out, no lock-in.
- Proof on hand: none yet (pre-launch, no testimonials or press). Worth gathering early — a few beta-dealer quotes or a real per-show profit screenshot would carry more weight than any adjective.

## Brand Personality

A no-nonsense pro tool: dependable, fast, straight-talking. The voice is plainspoken and specific, with the quiet confidence of software built by someone who actually works the table — it names shows, tables, cons, and cash flow the way a dealer does, and earns trust through detail rather than adjectives. The feeling to evoke is trust and relief ("this finally gets my world"), not excitement or hype. It respects the reader's time and never oversells.

## Anti-references

Four lanes to stay out of, all called out explicitly by the owner: generic SaaS template (purple gradient hero, identical feature-card grid, stock illustrations — the "AI made that" landing page); crypto/hype energy (neon, hype words, countdown-timer pressure); childish TCG clip-art (cartoon mascots, foil-everything, sparkles aimed at kids); and corporate/sterile B2B (stock handshakes, jargon, faceless with no soul). CardOps should read as a purpose-built tool for a real trade, distinct from all four.

## Design Principles

- **Built by someone who works the table.** Credibility comes from specificity — shows, cons, per-show P&L, cash flow — not from adjectives like "powerful" or "seamless". Speak the dealer's language.
- **Reliability is the headline, not a feature bullet.** The emotional core is "the wifi died and I can't sell." Offline-first is the thing that earns the download; lead with the fear it removes.
- **Show, don't hype.** No countdown pressure, no growth-hack theatrics. Demonstrate real numbers and dependability. Anti-hype is the personality, so the design must carry conviction without shouting.
- **Respect the pro's time.** Dense, scannable, honest. A busy dealer should grasp what this is and why it's for them in seconds, on a phone.
- **Distinctive, not templated.** Actively avoid the four anti-reference lanes. Look like a tool made for one trade, not a landing page generated for any.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA. Body text ≥4.5:1 contrast against its background (watch muted text on the cream body), large text ≥3:1, visible keyboard focus states, full keyboard navigation, and a `prefers-reduced-motion` alternative for any motion. No specialized user needs known beyond meeting AA well.
